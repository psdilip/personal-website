If you own enough watches, I guess that just means you've got a lot of time on your hands.

Time is strange when you actually sit with it. You can't see it, feel it, or hear it, and yet it's in every thought you have. It structures the whole day — the alarm, the appointments, the meetings — and we still don't really understand it.

So here's the question I keep coming back to: do we actually understand time well enough to make the most of it?

**What we do know:** it only moves one direction, and it feels faster or slower depending entirely on context. Nobody's proven that it runs backward.

**What smarter people than me have said about it:** the philosopher Alexander Bard lays out the disagreement well. Parmenides and Einstein both treated time as an illusion. Plato defined it as constant change. Darwin's whole theory of evolution requires time to be real. Bohr pushed back on the idea that anything is predetermined, leaning instead on complexity that emerges over time. Different fields, wildly different answers.

**And yet, day to day:** we're all stressed about it anyway — deadlines, anxiety about hitting some invisible milestone by some invisible age, taking risks just to make something happen in time.

Michael Altshuler said it better than I can: *"The bad news is time flies. The good news is you are the pilot."*
